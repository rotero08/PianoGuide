Teaching yourself piano well comes down to two skills that beginners constantly confuse with each other: technique and sight-reading. Most people pour all their effort into one and quietly neglect the other, and then they stall. The approach here keeps both growing side by side. To see why that works, it helps to understand what each skill actually is, and where the two best-known ideas about training them come from.

## What technique actually is

Technique is not playing fast, and it is not playing difficult pieces. It is the ability to produce any sound you intend with the least possible effort. The clearest account of this comes from the pianist and scientist Chuan C. Chang, whose free book *Fundamentals of Piano Practice* argues that most technical walls are not limits of talent or strength but inefficient movements learned by accident and then drilled in until they feel permanent. 

Chang's central tool is hands-separate practice. You work each hand on its own, often at or even above the speed you eventually want, until the motion feels easy and relaxed. Because each hand is solving only its own problem, the arm never has to stiffen up to hold two unsolved problems together at once. That stiffness, which Chang traces to muscles fighting each other, is the thing that caps your speed and tires you out. Remove it and the ceiling lifts. This is also why the method asks you to stop the moment you feel tension creeping in, because the relaxed version of a motion is the one that consolidates into memory while you sleep <a class="cite-tag" href="/reference/library/#ref-walker" data-tip="Walker, M. P., et al. (2002). Sleep-dependent learning and motor cortical plasticity. Neuron, 34(1), 141-145.">[6]</a>.

## What sight-reading actually is

Sight-reading is the ability to sit down with a piece of written music you have never seen and play it straight through, reading both staves in real time, without stopping. It is a genuinely separate skill from technique, and it is built in a different way. The most influential modern explanation comes from a working musician who writes on r/piano under the name u/Yeargdribble, whose advice has been repeated across the community for years. 

The core of it is that you get better at reading only by reading, and specifically by reading music easy enough that you can play it correctly on the very first attempt. The goal is to keep your eyes flowing forward and your hands answering by feel, so that notation turns into sound automatically rather than being decoded one symbol at a time. Reading hard music slowly does the opposite. It teaches you to stop, puzzle out each symbol, and stare at your hands, which is the habit that keeps most self-taught players from ever reading fluently.

## Where the two ideas collide

Stated plainly, Chang tells you to practise hands apart and assemble hard music slowly, while Yeargdribble tells you to play hands together and only on easy music. Read as universal rules, those two pieces of advice look like a flat contradiction, which is exactly why self-taught players tend to adopt one and dismiss the other. 

The contradiction disappears once you notice they are answering different questions. Chang is describing how to build the hands. Yeargdribble is describing how to build the eyes. Each is correct about its own job and harmful if you apply it to the other. Practise your hardest piece hands-together and slowly, and you drill tension straight into it, hitting a wall you cannot push past. Spend your reading time hands-apart on easy material, and your eyes never learn to track two staves at once. The two methods are not rivals. They are tools for two different tasks that beginners mistake for one.

## How this guide bridges them

From here on, the two tools get short names. Chang's hands-separate practice is **HS**, and Yeargdribble's hands-together reading is **HT**. The bridge between them is difficulty: you run both at once, on two separate pools of music. 

* **HS** goes on your hard repertoire, the pieces you are actively trying to master, worked at or above tempo so the hands stay relaxed. 
* **HT** goes on reading material kept two to three grades easier than what you can play, so the eyes stay fluent. 

Because the two pools never share a piece, the methods stop competing and begin to compound. HS keeps raising what your hands can do, HT keeps raising what your eyes can do, and the easy reading floor rises behind your playing without ever catching up to it. That single rule, HS on hard music apart and HT on easy music together, is what every level below is quietly scheduling for you.

## Where this curriculum comes from, and how it is sequenced

The level-by-level structure here is a <strong>combination of the two tracks</strong> above, and it leans most heavily on the open sight-reading curriculum published by <strong>Mr. Scarlet OnTheMoon</strong>, whose ordering of reading material runs underneath the whole path. 

The one change made on top of it is the order of emphasis. Rather than treating every level as a reading exercise first, the path <strong>leads with technique</strong>: the opening stage is about a relaxed body and a working ear, and <strong>sight-reading is introduced only once the hands are calm</strong>, from the early reading levels onward. After that the two grow together exactly as before, with the reading floor held a few grades below your playing. That is why, in every level below, the two halves sit side by side with <strong>technique on the left and sight-reading on the right</strong>: a standing reminder that your reading material is meant to stay well under the pieces your hands are actively building.

## What else this guide teaches, and where

Reading and technique are the spine, but a complete pianist needs more than those two, so each additional skill is introduced at the point in the path where it does the most good. 

* **Ear training** comes first and then runs through everything, because being able to hear a note in your head before you play it is what lets you notice and fix your own mistakes; the research behind starting there sits in <a href="/foundations/core-mindset/">The Core Mindset</a>. 
* **Keyboard geometry**, meaning scales, chords, and the shapes they make under the hand, is drilled steadily so your fingers can find their way by touch while your eyes stay on the page. 
* **Music theory and notation** are learned away from the keyboard first, in <a href="/skills/reading-music/">How To Read Music</a>, so that decoding never has to compete for attention while you are also trying to play. 
* **Harmony and lead-sheet playing** branch off once you can read simple intervals, giving you a way to accompany yourself and improvise rather than only reproduce what is printed. 

None of these are separate courses bolted on the side. They are strands of a single ability, introduced in an order that keeps the mental load low enough for your body to stay relaxed.

## Three traps the path is built to avoid

Most self-taught plateaus trace back to three specific mistakes. The whole curriculum is shaped to route around them.

## How to practise

Reading is mentally tiring, because your brain is processing new information every second, so practice is kept short, daily, and broken into focused blocks rather than stretched into rare marathons.

## Measuring yourself with graded syllabi

With no examiner, a widely suggested workaround is to borrow an external ruler. This guide follows that advice and uses the three major examination boards each publish a complete ladder of graded requirements, and you can measure yourself against that ladder at any time without ever paying for an exam. They work as a free standardised yardstick for difficulty.

<div class="board-grid">
    <div class="board-card">
        <h4>ABRSM</h4>
        <div class="region">UK, used worldwide</div>
        <p>The Associated Board of the Royal Schools of Music. Grades 1 to 8 plus diplomas, and the most widely recognised classical ladder in the world. Every grade specifies set pieces, scales, sight-reading, and aural tests, which together read like a ready-made self-assessment checklist.</p>
    </div>
    <div class="board-card">
        <h4>RCM</h4>
        <div class="region">Canada and USA</div>
        <p>The Royal Conservatory of Music Certificate Program, running from Preparatory through Level 10. Its repertoire lists are unusually detailed, which makes it the easiest board to use when you want to find pieces at one precise difficulty.</p>
    </div>
    <div class="board-card">
        <h4>Trinity College London</h4>
        <div class="region">UK, used worldwide</div>
        <p>An Initial grade plus Grades 1 to 8, with more freedom in choosing repertoire and a strong emphasis on musicianship. A useful second opinion when the other two feel too rigid for your taste.</p>

<div class="callout">
    <span class="callout-label">Using PianoSyllabus.com</span>
    <p>You do not need to memorise three separate grade lists. <a href="https://pianosyllabus.com/" target="_blank" rel="noopener">PianoSyllabus.com</a> is a free, searchable database that has gathered the official repertoire from ABRSM, RCM, Trinity, and several other boards into one place. You can look up a specific piece to see what grade each board assigns it, or browse a grade to pull a full list of pieces at that exact level. Use it two ways here. First, to place yourself: find pieces you can already play comfortably, and the grade they sit at is roughly your current playing level. Second, to feed the calibration rule below: once you know your playing grade, search two to three grades lower and use those results as your daily sight-reading pool, confident the difficulty is right.</p>
</div>

<div class="callout">
    <span class="callout-label">The calibration rule, a 2 to 3 grade gap</span>
    <p>Keep a deliberate gap of two to three grades between your physical playing level and your sight-reading material. If you can play RCM Level 5, read from RCM Level 1 or 2. Reading material has to stay easy enough that you can play it hands-together on the first pass, because that is what lets your eyes keep moving forward instead of collapsing into slow decoding. As your playing grade rises, the reading floor rises with it, but you never let the gap close.</p>
</div>

<div class="callout rose">
    <span class="callout-label">How to run a checkpoint</span>
    <p>Every level ends with a checkpoint tied to a board grade. To use it, pick the target grade, then test yourself on four things: play three list pieces at that grade accurately and musically, run the grade's required scales and arpeggios evenly, sight-read an unseen example written at the grade's reading standard, and pass a relative-pitch test on the <a href="https://apps.apple.com/us/app/functional-ear-trainer/id1088761926" target="_blank" rel="noopener">Functional Ear Trainer</a> at around 80 percent. Clear all four and you advance. Fall short on one and you hold there and drill it. Run the whole audit again about once a month.</p>
</div>
