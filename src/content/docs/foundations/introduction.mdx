Much of the thinking behind this path is owed to **Mr. Scarlet OnTheMoon**, whose open-access curriculum work inspired this interactive version. The path itself is a **combination of relaxed technique and sight-reading**, resequenced so that technique comes first and reading joins once the hands are calm; the reading order draws mostly from that sight-reading curriculum, with the full reasoning detailed in [How This Method Works](/foundations/methodology/). 

It also leans heavily on community leaders widely cited in these discussions, including **u/Yeargdribble** for ideas on relative-pitch audiation, and **Mayron Cole** for progressive preparatory reading frameworks. A full directory of the authors, platforms, and public libraries this guide draws on is listed at the bottom of this interface.

Press a key. That's the whole appeal of the piano: cause and effect, immediate and physical. This is your instrument for the year ahead. Find C, the white key just left of every group of two black keys, and hear what you're working toward.

This guide gathers the advice that comes up most often across **r/piano**, r/pianolearning, r/musictheory, long-running community classics, and the public-domain pedagogy that working teachers tend to recommend, and arranges it into a single ordered path. A recurring observation in those communities is that self-taught players often stall not for lack of effort but because they collect scattered tips with no sequence. The idea here is the opposite: one path, Pre-Staff to Level 7, sequenced so that physical technique and reading fluency grow together rather than fight each other.

<Callout bordered={true} accentColor="gold" label="The ultimate goal, what you will be able to do at the end">
  The aim of a path like this is to leave you a genuinely literate, self-sufficient pianist by the time you work through Level 7. Concretely, the target is to be able to: **sit at a piano and sight-read** intermediate-to-advanced classical scores, with multiple voices, ledger lines, ornaments, and key changes, hands-together on a first pass; **play with relaxed, tension-free technique**, using hands-separate practice to clear fast runs, octaves, and polyrhythms without strain; **open a lead sheet or chord chart and accompany yourself** by ear, building patterns on the fly; **hear and audiate** music well enough to catch your own mistakes; and, most importantly, **teach yourself any new piece** using a repeatable deliberate-practice loop, so you can keep improving for life. Reading, technique, harmony, and ear are treated here not as four separate courses but as four strands of one skill, braided together level by level.
</Callout>

Each of the levels opens with its own smaller goal, exactly what *that* level adds to the picture, so you always know what you're working toward and why.

## The rising tide of self-taught musicians

The pandemic years turned a quiet hobby into a movement. Piano sales climbed, online platforms reported record sign-ups, and a generation discovered that you don't need an expensive weekly lesson to start making music. One online learning service reported its new-user base growing several times over from early 2020 onward. The same "teach yourself" ethos that reshaped coding and language learning arrived at the keyboard, but the piano is unusually complex, and self-direction without structure tends to stall. That is exactly the gap this method closes.

## The myth of the "natural" musician

For decades, playing an instrument was chalked up to innate talent. The research says otherwise. Anders Ericsson, who pioneered the study of expert performance, argued that **deliberate practice, not talent, is the real engine of mastery**; his book *Peak* dismantles the idea that expert performance reflects fixed, untrainable abilities [8](/reference/library/#ref-8). That shift is empowering: it means the ceiling is set by how you practice, not by what you were born with. The whole architecture of this method is built on deliberate practice: isolating the exact bottleneck, solving it slowly and tension-free, then rebuilding speed.

## Building a foundation: the basics

Before complex pieces or improvisation, three foundations come first:
* **Music theory:** Learn to read the staff, time signatures, and key signatures, with interactive lessons at [musictheory.net](https://www.musictheory.net) and [Teoria.com](https://www.teoria.com) away from the keys.
* **Posture and technique:** Develop a relaxed seat, level wrists, and a gentle hand curve before drilling notes (see Pre-Staff for Alan Belkin's arm-weight drop).
* **Dexterity:** Simple finger exercises and scales build the spatial map your hands need. 

Master these and everything above them gets easier.

## Why technique comes first here

This guide combines several sources, but it makes one deliberate choice about order: **technique before sight-reading.** Almost every self-taught stall traces back to tension that set in during the first weeks, so the curriculum opens with two short, reading-free levels — [Technique 1](/technique/technique-1/) and [Technique 2](/technique/technique-2/). You do no notation in them at all. You simply build a calm body and hands that obey, so that when reading begins, your physical setup is never the thing holding you back.

Only after those two foundations do you enter the **Sight-Reading Curriculum**, Pre-Staff through Level 7. That graded reading path — its levels, its resource lists, and its benchmark test pieces — is adapted from **Mr. Scarlet OnTheMoon's sight-reading chart**. Every reading resource and every benchmark you meet in those levels comes from that work, kept together in the sight-reading track and clearly separate from technique. Inside each sight-reading level, the left side is the reading material; the right side is only the small amount of supporting technique that level needs.

<Callout bordered={true} accentColor="gold" label="The architectural insight">
  A point experienced self-learners tend to converge on is that the strongest piano education is rarely any single resource; it is a deliberate combination. The pattern they describe looks like this: build a structured foundation from a method book, use a small, trusted tool set (Functional Ear Trainer, Complete Rhythm Trainer, Sightread.dev, musictheory.net, Teoria.com, Anki) for feedback and theory, pull scores from public libraries like IMSLP, watch a classical channel such as Nahre Sol or Dr. Josh Wright (or Julian Bradley for jazz, Bill Hilton for chord-based playing) for off-keyboard demonstration, track progress against a graded syllabus, and practise with intention along the lines Ericsson describes. This guide simply tries to assemble that stack into one ordered path.
</Callout>

## Measuring yourself without a teacher

With no examiner, a widely suggested workaround is to borrow an external ruler. This guide follows that advice and uses the three major graded syllabi, **ABRSM**, **RCM**, and **Trinity College London**, as a free "ghost curriculum" to calibrate your level and decide when to advance. Each level below ends with a **✓ Checkpoint** tied to a grade, with concrete self-evaluation steps. The full explanation of how the boards work and how to run a checkpoint lives in [How This Method Works](/foundations/methodology/).

## The mandatory spine, and the optional branches

Not everything in this guide is meant to be done by everyone, in order, all at once. The people it draws on tend to describe one **required spine** that almost every self-learner is advised to follow, plus several **optional branches** you can pick up when you are ready, depending on the kind of player you want to become.

* **Mandatory Spine (The reading & technique spine):** Technique 1 and 2 first, then Pre-Staff through Level 7. This is the one path nearly everyone in these communities is pointed toward: relaxed technique built first, then fluent sight-reading grown on top of it. Supporting tools like [How To Read Music](/skills/reading-music/), [Rhythm, Clefs & Decoding](/skills/rhythm-clefs/), [Keyboard Geometry](/skills/scales-chords/), and [Play By Ear](/skills/ear-training/) feed in exactly where each level instructs.
* **Optional Branches (The harmony & keyboard branch):** If you also want to accompany singers, play pop and rock, or improvise rather than only read, this branch is for you:
  * **Lead Sheets** — playing a melody from chord symbols (unlocks after Level 1).
  * **Comping & Styles** — turning blocked chords into rhythmic accompaniment vamps (unlocks after Level 2).
  * **Modern / Jazz Harmony** — extended chords, sevenths, ninths, and lead sheet alterations (unlocks after Level 4).
