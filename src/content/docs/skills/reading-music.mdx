Before you practice sight-reading at the keys, ensure you understand the basic logic of notation. **Our goal is to learn notation rules away from the keyboard first,** so you don't have to think about them during practice.

<Callout bordered={true} accentColor="rose" label="Why we decode away from the keys: Sweller (1988)">
  Two staves, two clefs, ten fingers and a pedal blow past working memory's 4–7 item limit. Calculating individual pitch letters _while_ hitting motor targets is a classic split-attention overload [6](/reference/library/#ref-6), and that overload shows up physically as locked wrists and shoulders. Learning notation rules first, and reading by interval rather than letter, keeps load under the tension threshold.
</Callout>

<Callout bordered={true} accentColor="rose" label="Read ahead, not at the cursor: Goebl & Palmer (2008)">
  Skilled readers sweep their eyes 1–2 beats ahead of their hands (saccades), pre-loading the next coordinates so touch can guide exact placement [9](/reference/library/#ref-9). Falling-note apps force the eyes into "cursor lock," suppressing those forward saccades and blocking real spatial mapping. Always read relative vertical distances on a static grand staff.
</Callout>
