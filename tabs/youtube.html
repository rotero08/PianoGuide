Discovery, not practice

YouTube Masterclasses

The top of the YouTube piano space is genuinely excellent, but the algorithm rewards engagement rather than pedagogy, so a Juilliard graduate and a self-taught chord player can both have 500k subscribers. Below, sorted by what each channel actually does best. Full credit to every creator.

The Screen / Device Trap

Screens split your attention. Following Anders Ericsson's Peak principles, real deliberate practice happens with a printed, physical score on the music stand and every electronic device kept at a distance. Constantly returning to a phone or tablet to pause, rewind, and squint at hand positions turns the device into a distraction rather than a tool. Use YouTube for what it is uniquely good at, namely off-keyboard visual demonstration, discovery, and inspiration, and then close it before you practise. You will learn a piece far faster with a printed sheet and nothing else in front of you.

How to use this list

Pick one classical and one chord/jazz channel that match your goals, watch for demonstration and inspiration, then return to your printed scores and the levels above. Discovery on screen, practice on paper.
