Level 0: Standard Linear Reading

Your goal for this level

Read single notes on the grand staff with both hands together in a fixed five-finger (C) position, recognising intervals by their shape rather than spelling out every letter, while staying physically relaxed.

The five-line staff arrives. To keep the mental load low, your hands stay locked in one position, so almost all of your attention can go to the new job of tracking two staves at once.

Sight-Reading the eyes · hands-together

Stationary five-finger reading with no shifts and no leaps, just two clefs moving together.

    Kravchuk: 354 Reading Exercises in C Free The free fixed-position reading catalog to establish coordinate hands-together tracking.

    Michael Kravchuk, a performance graduate who publishes his books free as PDFs, wrote these specifically to fix the most common beginner flaw of neglecting the left hand. Every six-bar exercise keeps both hands in a fixed C position, so by removing all shifts and leaps it frees your whole attention to learn the one new skill of reading two clefs together.

    Practice plan
    Read three to five new exercises a day with the three-repeat rule. Done when you can sight-read about 30 of them smoothly on the first try.

Technique Support

Develop key decoding away from the bench to prevent working memory overload.

    Georges Dandelot: Manuel Pratique Paid Book Speak note names aloud away from the keys to isolate visual decoding.

    A classic note-reading drill book. The method is to speak note names aloud with a metronome at a slow tempo, away from the piano, which separates pure visual decoding from the job of moving your hands and stops your working memory from overloading.

    Practice plan
    Five minutes daily at 60 BPM. Done when you read a full grand-staff line with no hesitation.

    Westenra: Read Music FAST (Part 1) Paid Course Landmark and interval reading maps up to a fifth.

    A focused course built around landmark reading. Instead of the slow routine of recalling a mnemonic and counting lines, it anchors your eyes on reference notes such as Bass C, Middle C, and Treble C, then teaches you to read the distance from them straight into a finger span.

    Practice plan
    Ten minutes daily; Part 1 only for now. Done when you name any interval up to a fifth instantly.

How to use these benchmarks & calibrate difficulty

A benchmark is a short, real piece, not an exercise. Its only job is to prove that the reading you built at this level actually transfers to music before you move up. Do not drill it like repertoire.

  1. Listen to the recording once so you know the target sound.
  2. Sight-read the score cold, hands together, slow but steady — no stopping to fix notes.
  3. Pass: You get through it on the first or second attempt with a steady pulse and without watching your hands.
  4. Stall: If three honest attempts still fall apart, you are not ready. Don't grind it — drop back, read easier material, then return. (This is the chart’s "New Game +".)

Measuring your reading level via PianoSyllabus: Go to the PianoSyllabus search form. Enter a piece you currently play comfortably with correct phrasing. Check its normalized grade. If it is listed as RCM/ABRSM Grade 3, that represents your current playing ceiling. Subtract 2 or 3 grades to find your sight-reading band (Grade 1). Pull three unseen pieces from Grade 1 and attempt to sight-read them. If you read them with a steady pulse on the first pass, your coordinates are fully aligned [1.4].

The free Mayron Cole Audition Book page is the quick pass/fail gate. Passing this level lines up roughly with ABRSM / RCM Grade 1 reading.
