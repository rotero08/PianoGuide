Level 1 · vertical chunking

Level 1: Intervals & Chord Shapes

Your goal for this level

Read two notes at once and simple triads as single visual shapes, and play your first chord-symbol lead sheets, which connects printed reading to playing harmony at the keyboard.

Instead of one note at a time, your brain now learns to grab whole vertical shapes. This is also where the harmony track joins the main path for good.

Sight-Reading the eyes · hands-together

Graded reading of two-note intervals and simple triads, kept well below your physical limit and played with both hands.

    Bartok: Mikrokosmos, Book 1Real graded music for interval chunking.

    Mikrokosmos is a famous set of 153 progressive pieces Bartok wrote to teach reading and technique from the very beginning, free on IMSLP. The first book stays in simple intervals, so it gives you real, musical material to practise chunking two notes at once rather than dry exercises.

    Practice plan
    Read two or three pieces a day with the three-repeat rule. Done when you chunk intervals without spelling letters.

Technique the hands · hands-separate

Learn chords as physical shapes your hand can grab by feel, which is the foundation of the whole harmony track.

    Jeremy Siskind: Jazz Piano Fundamentals (Ch. 1 to 4)Chords as hand shapes, not stacked notes.

    Siskind is an award-winning jazz pianist and educator whose method is prized for teaching chords as geometric hand shapes rather than stacks of separate notes. Building triads and their inversions with the smallest possible motion means your hand learns to land a chord as one gesture.

    Practice plan
    Fifteen minutes daily. Done when you build any triad and its inversions by feel.
    Open resource ↗
    Julie Lind: Graded Lead SheetsMelody plus chord symbols to play from.

    Free lead sheets, meaning a melody with chord symbols above it rather than every note written out. Reading a symbol and shaping the chord by feel under a melody is the practical payoff of the chord work, and the doorway into accompanying yourself.

    Practice plan
    One lead sheet a day. Done when you can shape chords under a melody directly from symbols.

How the two halves connect hereA printed two-note interval and a written chord symbol are really the same spatial skill, because both turn one visual cue straight into a hand shape. Reading Bartok trains your eye to chunk intervals, while Siskind trains your hand to own those chunks, so together they stop you from spelling out notes one letter at a time.

Checkpoint

Checkpoint, am I ready for the next level?

≈ ABRSM / RCM Grade 1 to 2 (reading)

Confirm that you read vertically, in shapes, rather than one letter at a time.

  1. Read three new Level 1 reading pieces cleanly on the first or second attempt.
  2. Play the major and minor triads of any key in all inversions by feel.
  3. Play three lead sheets hands-together with smooth left-hand inversions.
  4. Run a quick relative-pitch check on the Functional Ear Trainer at around 80 percent.

When all four hold, move to Level 2. The harmony track now continues in parallel for good.

Optional branch unlocked

Lead Sheets is now available. Once you can read simple intervals, most teachers say you are ready to start playing from chord symbols. It is optional, run it alongside the spine if you want to accompany yourself or improvise.
