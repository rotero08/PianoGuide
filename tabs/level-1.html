Level 1: Intervals & Chord Shapes

Your goal for this level

Read two notes at once and simple triads as single visual shapes, and play your first chord-symbol lead sheets, which connects printed reading to playing harmony at the keyboard.

Instead of one note at a time, your brain now learns to grab whole vertical shapes. This is also where the harmony track joins the main path for good.

Sight-Reading the eyes · hands-together

Graded reading of two-note intervals and simple triads, kept well below your physical limit and played with both hands.

    Bartok: Mikrokosmos, Book 1 IMSLP Recommended Real graded music to practice chunking two notes at once rather than dry exercises.

    Mikrokosmos is a famous set of 153 progressive pieces Bartok wrote to teach reading and technique from the very beginning, free on IMSLP. The first book stays in simple intervals, so it gives you real, musical material to practise chunking two notes at once rather than dry exercises.

    Practice plan
    Read two or three pieces a day. Done when you chunk intervals without spelling letters.

Technique Support

Learn chords as physical shapes your hand can grab by feel, which is the foundation of the harmony track.

    Jeremy Siskind: Jazz Piano (Ch. 1 to 4) Paid Book Triads and inversions structured as geographic gestures rather than stacked letters.

    Siskind is an award-winning jazz pianist and educator whose method is prized for teaching chords as geometric hand shapes rather than stacks of separate notes. Building triads and their inversions with the smallest possible motion means your hand learns to land a chord as one gesture.

How to use these benchmarks & calibrate difficulty

A benchmark is a short, real piece, not an exercise. Its only job is to prove that the reading you built at this level actually transfers to music before you move up. Do not drill it like repertoire.

  1. Listen to the recording once so you know the target sound.
  2. Sight-read the score cold, hands together, slow but steady — no stopping to fix notes.
  3. Pass: You get through it on the first or second attempt with a steady pulse and without watching your hands.
  4. Stall: If three honest attempts still fall apart, you are not ready. Don't grind it — drop back, read easier material, then return. (This is the chart’s "New Game +".)

Measuring your reading level via PianoSyllabus: Go to the PianoSyllabus search form. Enter a piece you currently play comfortably with correct phrasing. Check its normalized grade. If it is listed as RCM/ABRSM Grade 3, that represents your current playing ceiling. Subtract 2 or 3 grades to find your sight-reading band (Grade 1). Pull three unseen pieces from Grade 1 and attempt to sight-read them. If you read them with a steady pulse on the first pass, your coordinates are fully aligned [1.4].

The Era Repertoire Rule: You only need to successfully sight-read one of the era benchmark pieces to clear a level and advance. The five historical eras (Baroque, Classical, Romantic, C20, and Modern) are provided as options to give you flexibility. If one piece isn't clicking, try another! However, returning to these benchmarks later and passing all five is recommended to prove your reading is general and versatile rather than pattern-matched to a single composer.

The five era pieces (Baroque through Modern) test the same reading across different musical styles, so passing all five means your reading is general rather than pattern-matched to one composer. The free Mayron Cole Audition Book page is the quick pass/fail gate. Passing this level lines up roughly with ABRSM / RCM Grade 1–2 reading.
