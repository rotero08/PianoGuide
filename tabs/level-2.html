Level 2 · two independent voices

Level 2: Voices & Keyboard Geometry

Your goal for this level

Read and play two independent melodic lines at the same time, read key signatures as keyboard terrain rather than as arithmetic, and navigate scales and triads as an automatic map under the hand.

Real polyphony begins, meaning both hands play distinct lines instead of melody plus accompaniment. That demands a genuine internal map of the keyboard.

How the two halves connect hereIndependent voices need a map that the page does not give you. Learning key signatures away from the keys, plus daily scale geometry, means your left hand can hold its own line by touch while your eyes track the right. The old advice to stop staring at your hands only becomes possible once that geometry is automatic.

Practice rhythm

Daily · about 45 min

Ten minutes of scales and triads in the day's key, ten minutes of Westenra key-signature reading, and twenty-five minutes of Kunz canons, hands separate then together, slow and smooth.

Weekly

Read a new Bartok or Dunhill two-voice piece cold, then isolate the two hardest bars.

Checkpoint

Checkpoint, am I ready for the next level?

≈ ABRSM / RCM Grade 2 (reading)

Confirm genuine two-hand independence while reading key signatures.

  1. Read a new two-voice piece hands-together, keeping both lines even.
  2. Play scales and triads of the week's key smoothly and tension-free with your eyes up.
  3. Read ledger notes up to three lines beyond each staff without hesitation.
  4. Confirm your reading material is still two to three grades under your playing level. Check grades here.

When all four hold, move to Level 3.

Level 2 benchmarks

How to use these benchmarks

A benchmark is a short, real piece, not an exercise. Its only job is to prove that the reading you built this level actually transfers to music before you move up. Do not drill it like repertoire.

  1. Listen to the recording once so you know the target sound.
  2. Sight-read the score cold, hands together, slow but steady — no stopping to fix notes.
  3. Pass: you get through it on the first or second attempt with a steady pulse and without watching your hands.
  4. Stall: if three honest attempts still fall apart, you are not ready. Don’t grind it — drop back, read easier material, then return. (This is the chart’s “New Game +”.)

The five era pieces (Baroque through Modern) test the same reading across different musical styles, so passing all five means your reading is general rather than pattern-matched to one composer. The free Mayron Cole Audition Book page is the quick pass/fail gate. Passing this level lines up roughly with around ABRSM / RCM Grade 2–3.
