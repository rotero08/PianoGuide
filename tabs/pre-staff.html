Pre-Staff: Arm Weight & the Ear

Your goal for this level

Sit relaxed, find any key by feel without looking down, match a played pitch with your voice, and play simple five-finger patterns hands-together from directional (arrow) notation.

Before the staff appears, you build the two things everything else rests on: a relaxed body and a working ear. Reading at this stage is purely directional, just arrows showing up or down, so all of your attention can go to relaxation and learning the geography of the keys.

Sight-Reading the eyes · hands-together

Graphic and arrow notation only, with no five-line staff yet. The eyes simply learn that higher means move right and lower means move left.

Technique the hands · hands-separate

Build the relaxed motion you will use forever, and start the ear that will later catch every reading mistake. Posture lives in one place in this guide, so its full set of demonstrations is in the Posture & Biomechanics tab.

    Posture & arm weightAll posture lives in one tab; start with Belkin.

    All of the posture and arm-weight instruction lives in the Posture & Biomechanics tab so it is never scattered across the guide. Start with Alan Belkin's short arm-drop demonstration there, because letting a relaxed arm fall into the key rather than pressing with the fingers is the single habit that prevents early tension.

    Practice plan
    Watch once, then drop each hand daily. Done when your wrist stays loose and springy on every note.
    Functional Ear TrainerDaily relative-pitch app.

    A free app that drills relative pitch by asking you to name notes by how they pull toward a home key, rather than as isolated intervals. This is the fastest known way to build the inner sense of pitch that lets you hear a mistake the instant you play it. Its home, with setup notes, is the Play By Ear tab.

    Practice plan
    Ten minutes daily. Done when you reach about 80 percent on scale-degree sets within one octave of C.
    First Steps to Ear Training (William Curtis)Sing back the pitch a key plays.

    A short, structured book whose first chapter has you sing back the exact pitch a key plays. Forcing your voice to match a tone is what proves the pitch is genuinely in your head, because a sound you can sing is a sound your hands can later be corrected against.

    Practice plan
    Five minutes of Chapter 1 daily. Done when you can match any single key by voice.

How the two halves connect hereAt this stage reading and technique are almost the same lesson. You drop each hand separately onto the keys to feel weight and release, then play the easy arrow patterns with both hands while singing the pitch you expect to hear. The ear flags a wrong note, and the relaxed arm lets you correct it without stiffening, which is the loop the entire method runs on later.

≈ ABRSM / RCM Preparatory (pre-Grade 1)

You are not being graded yet. You are confirming that the physical and aural base is in place before notation arrives.

  1. With your eyes closed, find any named natural key across four octaves by feel.
  2. Hum and match five random single pitches the piano plays, with no tracking errors.
  3. Play five hands-together arrow patterns without looking down at the keys.
  4. Score around 80 percent on a Functional Ear Trainer scale-degree set: open the trainer.

Pass all four and move on to Level 0. To see roughly where you sit against the graded boards, use PianoSyllabus.com.
